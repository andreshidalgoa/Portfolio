Compliant Gripper for Micro-Assembly

Research intern — design, simulation, fabrication & testing

Brief: design a gripper achieving parallel motion for micro-assembly, derived from rigid-body kinematics — then validate it through simulation and physical testing.

Parametric ANSYS simulations — amplification ratio vs joint geometry

FDM-fabricated gripper and custom test bench — physical validation

Translated a rigid-body Scott–Russell mechanism into a compliant design by mapping joint angular displacements to flexure geometry. Ran parametric ANSYS structural simulations varying joint thickness, geometry, and starting angle to characterize their effect on amplification ratio and parasitic Z-axis displacement. Manufactured the gripper and a custom universal testing bench entirely via FDM. Validated simulation results through physical testing and optical motion tracking.

✓ Simulated amplification ratio: 2.4 — experimental: 3.16. Deviation systematically analyzed and attributed to FDM joint geometry tolerances and boundary condition assumptions.

Master's Thesis Research

MADIT Laboratory — UNAM

Direct Polymer Additive Tooling for Low-Volume Cup-Drawing Production

Researcher — design, fabrication, characterization & simulation

2024 – 2026
Research question: can 3D-printed polymer tooling substitute conventional metal dies for low-volume sheet-metal cup-drawing, and what are the actual limits — in geometry accuracy, tool life, and failure modes?

Designing and 3D-printing polymer tools for sheet-metal cup-drawing operations, then running systematic experimental drawing trials to characterize tool performance, geometry accuracy, and failure modes. Material properties are determined experimentally via tensile testing on a Shimadzu universal testing machine, feeding directly into ANSYS structural simulation models. Operating industrial AM equipment at MADIT Lab: Stratasys Fortus 400MC (FDM) and EOS P100 (SLS), including material loading, print parameter selection, and post-processing. The work aims to establish polymer additive tooling as a viable low-cost, fast-lead-time alternative to conventional tooling for low-volume runs.

Expected thesis defense: 2026.
